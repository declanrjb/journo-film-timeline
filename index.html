Journalism in the Movies

In our Winter Term class, we studied how decades of cinema have shaped Americans’ views of the press. In the timeline below, we share what we learned from a dozen films spanning more than 80 years.

1940

His Girl Friday, directed by Howard Hawks, is a zany 1940s comedy starring Cary Grant and Rosalind Russell as snide divorcees Walter and Hildy that happen to work for the same newspaper. Hildy is primed to retire and begin a life with her new husband; Walter desperately wants her back and knows all of her weak spots, especially her vulnerability to a good story. What follows from there is a fast-paced whirlwind of murderers, policemen, mothers-in-law, corrupt local government, counterfeit money, and extortion. His Girl Friday is one of the most famous journalism movies of all time, and for good reason - it deftly explores themes such as exploitation in the industry, misogyny, and the power compelling journalism possesses to sway the public.

Constructive and destructive journalism in His Girl Friday

1976

All the President's Men

Covering what initially seems like a simple break in at the Democratic National Headquarters, Washington Post reporters Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein slowly unravel a massive government conspiracy to sabotage the electoral opponents of President Richard Nixon.

Network

1998

Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas

A non-narrative, psychedelic adaptation of Hunter S. Thompson’s Gonzo journalistic novel of the same name. The film follows a journalist, Raoul Duke, and his attorney, Dr. Gonzo, going to Las Vegas with simple journalistic intent that soon devolves into a psychological, drug fueled exploration of the city and the nation.

1999

Spanning 170 years of American History, Stanley Nelson Jr.’s documentary The Black Press details the role of African American-operated newspapers as voices for their communities, spreading important news, events, and achievements in a time when mainstream media did not represent them.

Giving voice to the voiceless in The Black Press

2003

Shattered Glass

Stephen Glass, a young, popular reporter for The New Republic Magazine, is finally caught in his long-time act of publishing fantastical but completely made-up stories.

2014

Nightcrawler is a darkly comedic crime thriller directed by Dan Gilroy that captures the fictional rise of Louis Bloom, a sociopathic criminal turned video journalist who goes to morally questionable lengths to climb up the ladder and deliver graphic videos to a struggling news company. The film portrays the modern journalist as a parasite who sensationalizes violence for monetary gain.

Capitalism and the media in Nightcrawler

2015

Spotlight, based on a real story, follows the four reporters on The Boston Globe’s investigative reporting team, Spotlight, as they uncover a large network of child abuse by Catholic priests that has been systemically covered up by the church. What starts as a small story covering one case slowly builds up as the Spotlight team uncovers a decades-long cover up and enabling of abusive priests by the Church through transferring the priests to different locations.

The press as an institution in Spotlight

2020

A Thousand Cuts

The documentary directed by Ramona S. Diaz captures Maria Ressa and the journalists of Rappler, an online news site, as they uncover the tragic killings of the Filipino youth as part of President Duterte’s war on drugs. It highlights the challenges that the press face under Duterte’s regime as it becomes increasingly authoritarian.

2023

Lee, directed by Ellen Kuras remembers Lee Miller, 20th century photojournalist and model, as portrayed by Kate Winslet. In a fictional interview set in 1977 Lee recalls her experiences as a photographer for Vogue magazine during World War II. Lee reminisces on her life prior to the war and her subsequent move to England after meeting her partner, Roland Penrose, played by Alexander Skarsgård. As the war progresses across Europe and England is eventually pulled in, Lee progressively becomes more involved through her photography. Initially used to promote protective gear and other war efforts through Vogue, Lee ultimately pushes her way to the war front in France and finally Germany.
